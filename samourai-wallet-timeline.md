# Samourai Wallet case documents

*Archives and timeline of the public court documents from the Samourai Wallet case.*

January 22, 2024

[Industry Letter](https://web.archive.org/web/20240124170556/https://blog.samourai.is/content/files/2024/01/Section-311-Mixing-Transactions-Designation-NPRM-Comment-Letter.pdf)

Samourai Wallet, in response to FinCEN’s Notice of Proposed Rule Making (NPRM) on mixing transactions, opposed the designation of "convertible virtual currency (CVC) mixing" as a class of transactions subject to Section 311 special measures. The letter argues that the proposed rule is overbroad, legally questionable, and technologically ill-informed, as it conflates a technical process with a business model and targets open-source privacy tools used by legitimate users. Samourai contends that FinCEN already has effective tools to investigate illicit activity and should instead use its traditional, targeted approach—designating specific bad actors under Section 311—rather than imposing sweeping surveillance on privacy-enhancing technologies that are essential for cybersecurity and constitutional privacy rights. FinCEN is required by law to consider all submitted comments and the agency has not moved forward with this proposal since Samourai Wallet and 25 other Bitcoin companies co-signed this letter.

March 20, 2024

[Decentralized Whirlpool Blog Post](https://web.archive.org/web/20240324202723/https://blog.samourai.is/decentralized-whirlpool-stage-1/)

The announcement of Decentralized Whirlpool: Stage 1 signifies a major step toward enhancing the resilience and privacy of Samourai's coinjoin service by eliminating reliance on a single, centralized coordinator server. By migrating client-coordinator communication to Soroban, an encrypted, Tor-based protocol, the update enables automatic discovery of multiple coordinator servers, minimizing downtime if one goes offline — a system dubbed "Whack-A-Mole." While the user experience remains unchanged, this backend shift strengthens the protocol against censorship and service disruption, bolsters user anonymity by removing static clearnet addresses, and lays the foundation for a more decentralized future, with older non-Soroban client versions eventually being phased out.
